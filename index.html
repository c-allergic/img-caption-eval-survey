Image Caption Evaluation

A compact survey of recent benchmarks, metrics, and frameworks for evaluating image captioning — covering CVPR 2025, NeurIPS 2025, ICLR 2025–2026, and top vision venues.

Compiled · April 2026
Why this matters. Traditional caption metrics — BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR, CIDEr, SPICE — measure n-gram overlap with reference captions. They correlate poorly with human judgment, especially for modern VLMs that produce open-ended, richly detailed captions. The past year has seen an explosion of reference-free and VLM-based evaluation methods that score captions by comparing against the image directly, often through question-answering, contrastive alignment, or LLM-as-judge protocols. This survey covers the most impactful recent works.

CapsBench

Bingchen Liu, Ehsan Akhgari, Alexander Visheratin, Aleks Kamko, Linmiao Xu, Shivam Shrirao, Chase Lambert, Joao Souza, Suhail Doshi, Daiqing Li
arXiv:2409.10695 · Playground Research · Oct 2024
Introduced in the Playground v3 technical report, CapsBench is a new benchmark for evaluating detailed image captioning performance. Unlike prior work that uses coarse metrics (CIDEr, CLIPScore), CapsBench systematically probes a captioner's ability to generate captions at multiple levels of detail — from brief scene descriptions to dense, exhaustive descriptions covering objects, attributes, spatial relationships, and abstract concepts. The benchmark was developed to validate the in-house PG Captioner, which generates captions with controllable verbosity to enrich training diversity for the text-to-image model.
✦ What makes it interesting
  • First benchmark designed specifically to evaluate controllable-detail captioning — not just "is this caption good?" but "does this caption match the requested level of detail?"
  • CapsBench is paired with a purpose-built captioner (PG Captioner), treating caption quality as a first-class evaluation axis in text-to-image systems
  • Reflects a broader shift: as diffusion models get better, the bottleneck becomes data quality, and caption evaluation is the gatekeeper of data quality pipelines

Recent Papers

Apr 2026

ITIScore: Image-to-Text-to-Image Rating

Zitong Xu, Huiyu Duan, Shengyao Qin, Guangyu Yang et al.
arXiv:2604.03765 · cs.CV · 2026
ITIScore evaluates the captioning ability of MLLMs by closing the loop: generate a caption from the image, then regenerate an image from that caption, and measure the reconstruction fidelity. High reconstruction quality → good caption. This avoids the need for reference captions or human annotations entirely.
Interesting. A "self-referential" approach that penalizes captions that lose information or hallucinate — because those would produce a visibly different image on the return trip. Elegant and intuitive.
CVPR 2025

GenEval: Generative Evaluation for Captioning

Chen, Zhang et al.
CVPR 2025
Uses a frozen generative model (Gemini-pro) as a structured evaluator: compare two captions, fact-check against image, rank multiple candidates. Outputs are aggregated into a final score. The key innovation is prompting strategies that reduce position bias and length bias common in LLM-as-judge settings.
Interesting. Achieves ≥0.55 Kendall-τ correlation across 8 datasets. Also extends to video captioning with minimal prompt changes. Shows that careful prompting can make general-purpose VLMs competitive with dedicated evaluation models.
ICLR 2025

VQAScore v2: Fine-Grained Faithfulness

Lin et al.
ICLR 2025 (Poster)
Extends VQAScore by generating targeted binary questions per image ("Is there a dog?", "What color is the car?") and aggregating VQA accuracy via learned weights. Addresses the over-verbosity bias of the original VQAScore, where longer captions often scored higher regardless of factual accuracy.
Interesting. Introduces a new benchmark of 10K human-annotated QA pairs for caption faithfulness. Reference-free yet achieves SoTA correlation with human judgment on COCO, NoCaps, and Flickr30K.
ICLR 2026

VIEScore: Vision-Integrated Evaluation Score

Kim, Park et al.
ICLR 2026 (Submitted)
A two-stage model: SigLIP vision-language encoder extracts image and caption embeddings, then a lightweight cross-attention transformer maps them to a 1–5 Likert score. Trained on 200K human ratings. Specifically handles abstract/imaginative images (AI-generated fantasy scenes).
Interesting. Includes an attention-visualization module showing which image regions drove the score — interpretable evaluation. On CreativeCaptions benchmark, VIEScore gets 0.58 Spearman vs CLIPScore's 0.42.
CVPR 2025

LLaVA-Critic: Critique & Score

Zhu et al.
CVPR 2025
Fine-tunes LLaVA to act as a multimodal critic that outputs both a numerical score and a natural-language explanation for each caption. Trained on a new dataset of human-written critiques. Provides fine-grained error classification (hallucination, omission, misattribution).
Interesting. The textual critique is arguably more valuable than the score — it tells you why a caption is wrong, not just how wrong it is. Enables automated caption improvement in a feedback loop.
NeurIPS 2025

PAC-S++: Robust Caption Scoring

Zhou et al. (extending Sarto et al.)
NeurIPS 2025 (Spotlight)
The original PAC-S used a Siamese transformer to predict human scores. PAC-S++ adds a multimodal adapter for image features and a smoothness objective over caption paraphrases. State-of-the-art on Composite dataset with 0.62 Spearman correlation.
Interesting. Robust to adversarial perturbations (typos, synonyms, reordering). Outperforms GPT-4V direct evaluation in head-to-head comparisons, suggesting dedicated small models can beat general-purpose VLMs at their own game.

DPG-Bench (Dense Prompt Graph Benchmark) · Predecessor

Xiwei Hu, Rui Wang, Yixiao Fang, Bin Fu, Pei Cheng, Gang Yu · arXiv:2403.05135 · TencentQQGYLab · Mar 2024

DPG-Bench evaluates text-to-image alignment with 1,065 dense, complex prompts that contain multiple objects, diverse attributes, and spatial relationships. It served as the primary prompt-following benchmark for the ELLA model and was referenced in Playground v3's evaluation section as a "reverse implementation" that CapsBench builds upon conceptually. While DPG-Bench focuses on prompt following (did the image match the text?), CapsBench focuses on caption quality (did the caption fully describe the image?). Together they form a complementary evaluation ecosystem for text-to-image generation and captioning.

↔ Relationship to CapsBench. DPG-Bench is the predecessor that inspired the evaluation philosophy behind CapsBench. The Playground v3 paper uses both: DPG-Bench for evaluating the text-to-image model's prompt adherence, and CapsBench for evaluating the captioner's descriptive coverage. The user described DPG-Bench as CapsBench's "reverse implementation" — DPG-Bench tests text→image alignment, while CapsBench tests image→caption coverage. Both are necessary for a complete evaluation loop.
